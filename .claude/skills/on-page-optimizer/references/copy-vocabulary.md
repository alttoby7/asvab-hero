# Copy Vocabulary (4.7-tuned)

Reference for the subjective stages of on-page-optimizer — new H2 sections, meta descriptions, and gap-analysis prose. Loaded by `SKILL.md` (Voice and expertise section, Model calibration section) and `references/content-optimization.md` Optimization 3 (pre-flight ritual).

## The core problem this guide solves

Opus 4.7 reads body-copy requests literally. Asked to write "a comprehensive section about X," 4.7 produces sterile SEO-flavored prose: topic-restatement opening, informational filler, a generic conclusion. The result is schema-compliant (right length, keyword mentioned, no filler words from the explicit forbidden list) and still reads AI-written.

Four countermeasures this file enforces:

1. **Sterile defaults are explicitly forbidden** (§1). No *comprehensive, engaging, thorough, in-depth, valuable, actionable*.
2. **Specificity replacements for every forbidden word** (§2). The voice move is named so you know which kind of concrete term substitutes for each abstract one.
3. **Voice moves** — the reusable sentence and paragraph shapes an SEO copywriter reaches for (§3).
4. **Good/bad paired examples** for the two biggest drift spots: meta descriptions (§4) and new H2 sections (§5).

Plus a 7-item self-check (§6) and field notes from prior runs (§7).

## §1 Forbidden defaults (cut these from every draft)

### Body-copy forbidden

Every one of these replaces with a specific claim, not with a synonym. If you cannot produce the specific claim, the paragraph does not belong in the draft.

- **comprehensive, thorough, in-depth, detailed, exhaustive** — replace with a quantified claim ("covers the 12 subtopics competitors address," "3,200 words," "6 worked examples," "20 data points from the last 6 months"). If the page does not earn the quantified claim, do not use the abstraction either.
- **engaging, compelling, valuable, actionable, informative, helpful, insightful, useful** — cut. These describe the effect you want; say the cause that produces it (a number, a contrast, a named example, a dated claim, a proper noun).
- **natural, reader-friendly, well-structured, clear, cohesive, seamless** — these describe absence of problems. Replace with the positive structural feature (bulleted list under each H2, one-claim-per-paragraph, dated numbers instead of timeless assertions, contrast-and-claim openers).
- **quality, expertly-crafted, carefully-considered, well-researched** — cut. Show the research (cite the source, the date, the method) instead of claiming it.

### Connective-filler forbidden (at paragraph or sentence starts)

- *In today's fast-paced world…*
- *It's important to note that…*
- *With that being said…*
- *Moreover…* / *Furthermore…* / *Additionally…*
- *In conclusion…* / *To summarize…* / *All in all…*
- *Last but not least…*
- *When it comes to X…*
- *Whether you're a beginner or an expert…*
- *At the end of the day…*

Correction: drop the filler and let the claim lead. If transition is needed, use a concrete bridge ("A similar pattern shows up in…", "The trade-off here is…", "Here's the thing —").

### SEO-marketer-voice forbidden

- *leverage*
- *unlock the power of* / *harness the potential*
- *cutting-edge* / *state-of-the-art* / *next-generation*
- *best-in-class* / *world-class* / *top-tier*
- *game-changer* / *game-changing*
- *optimize for enhanced visibility*
- *drive traffic and engagement*
- *take your SEO to the next level*
- *dive deep into* / *explore the ins and outs of*

Correction: say the literal thing. Not "leverage our expertise" → "we've run 200+ campaigns; here's the one we'd pick for your situation." Not "take your SEO to the next level" → "move from position 12 to position 3."

## §2 Specificity-and-evidence replacements

The grammar of a good SEO section is specific, dated, and proper-noun-anchored. This table maps each forbidden default to the voice-move that replaces it and a concrete example.

| Instead of (forbidden default) | Write (specific replacement) | Voice move |
|---|---|---|
| "be thorough" | name 3 specific subtopics covered, with competitor coverage counts ("covers ages 8-16 weeks, socialization, and recall — the three subtopics all 5 top competitors include") | *numbered benchmark* |
| "valuable / actionable tips" | state the named method and one worked example ("the 30/30 recall method: 30 seconds of play, 30 seconds of recall practice, repeated 4x in a 10-minute session") | *named-method + example* |
| "engaging opening" | a number, a proper noun, or a contrarian claim in the first sentence | *specificity hook* |
| "in today's market" / "these days" | "in 2025," "as of March 2026," or the specific Google update ("after the March 2025 Helpful Content update") | *dated claim* |
| "many people wonder" | name the population ("parents of first-time puppies," "Edinburgh-based remote workers," "indie Astro sites under 10k monthly visits") | *named audience* |
| "natural-sounding content" | state the structural rule: "each paragraph carries one claim; every third paragraph breaks into a bulleted list or table" | *named structural rule* |
| "comprehensive coverage of X" | "the 12 subtopics that 4+ of the top 5 competitors cover, plus 2 subtopics only 1 competitor covers" | *coverage-count with source* |
| "expert-level insights" | cite the source inline ("per Google's Search Central docs, March 2025," "Rand Fishkin, Whiteboard Friday Oct 2024") | *cited source* |
| "dive deep into the details" | replace with the specific detail you're about to describe; the sentence should not promise depth, it should demonstrate it | *show-don't-tell* |

## §3 Voice moves — the reusable shapes

Each move is a sentence or paragraph pattern. Use 2-3 per new H2 section, not all of them. Over-using voice moves reads as affected.

### Contrast-and-claim

"Most guides say X. In practice, Y."

> *"Most SEO tools will tell you to stuff the keyword into the first 100 words. In practice, the keyword goes wherever the first substantive paragraph lands — which is usually inside a hook, not at the top."*

Used when a conventional SEO belief is about to be contradicted.

### Honest-admission disarm

Concede the obvious objection before making the claim.

> *"Yes, word count by itself doesn't matter. What matters is subtopic coverage — and longer pages tend to cover more subtopics, which is why the word-count correlation exists."*

Used when the reader is about to object; disarms before they do.

### Parenthetical aside

A short bracketed clause that adds a specific fact without slowing the sentence.

> *"The page ranked #3 (DR 34 — lower than competitors' average of 52) until the March update."*

Used to inject a dated or named-entity fact without breaking the flow.

### Implied-reader objection

Surface what the reader is thinking, then answer it.

> *"You might be wondering whether a keyword difficulty of 80 means this is impossible. It doesn't — it means you need backlinks too, not just on-page. KD 80 is where on-page alone stops being enough, not where ranking stops being possible."*

Used before a counter-intuitive claim.

### Callback sentence

Return to an earlier specific detail to close a loop.

> *"Remember the 3,200-word competitor from earlier? That page covers those same 12 subtopics we listed — which is why it's #1."*

Used in the final paragraph of a section or a page.

### Bullet-to-prose alternation

Rule of thumb: no more than 3 consecutive bullets without a prose paragraph; no more than 3 consecutive prose paragraphs without a bullet list or table. Every third structural element switches shape.

Used as a constant constraint on section drafting, not a one-off move.

## §5 New H2 section — good/bad paired examples

Each example is an excerpt (~150-200 words) from a hypothetical new section the optimizer adds.

### Example 1 — Subtopic: "How to choose a dog trainer in Edinburgh"

#### Bad (sterile default)

> ## How to Choose a Dog Trainer in Edinburgh
>
> Choosing the right dog trainer in Edinburgh is an important decision for every pet owner. There are many factors to consider when selecting a professional who can help your dog with their training needs. It's important to find someone who is experienced, qualified, and uses positive methods. A great dog trainer will work closely with you and your pet to achieve the best results possible.
>
> When looking for a dog trainer, make sure to do your research thoroughly. Check reviews, ask for recommendations, and ensure they are properly certified. The right trainer can make a huge difference in your dog's behavior and overall well-being. Take your time to find the perfect match for your specific needs.

**Why it's bad:** topic-restatement opening, no specifics, no proper nouns, no numbers, no dates. Every sentence uses a forbidden default (comprehensive, important, great, huge difference, perfect). Reads AI-written.

#### Good (specific)

> ## How to Choose a Dog Trainer in Edinburgh
>
> Four things decide whether a dog trainer actually helps: method, credential, class size, and whether they coach *you* or only the dog.
>
> **Method.** Reward-based (positive reinforcement, desensitisation, management) is now standard. If a trainer mentions "alpha rolls," "pack leadership," or uses a slip lead on a 12-week puppy, walk away — these are 2005 methods that lost consensus around 2015. The ABTC (Animal Behaviour and Training Council) only lists reward-based practitioners.
>
> **Credential.** ABTC register, IMDT (Institute of Modern Dog Trainers), or APDT (Association of Pet Dog Trainers) are the three that matter in the UK. "Certified dog trainer" without a named body is marketing.
>
> **Class size.** For puppy classes, 4-6 pups per trainer is the range. Above 8, individual attention is gone. Below 3, your pup learns nothing about dogs interacting with dogs — which is half the point.
>
> **Handler coaching.** The trainer's real job is to teach *you* the mechanics. If the trainer does all the work with the dog while you watch, you'll be back to square one on Monday morning. Ask on the first call: "How much will I be doing versus watching?"

**Why it's good:** number-up-front opener (*Four things decide…*), four named structural elements, three named credentials (ABTC, IMDT, APDT), a dated claim ("2005 methods that lost consensus around 2015"), a contrast-and-claim move (reward-based vs alpha rolls), and an implied-reader-objection move (the "ask on the first call" close). No forbidden defaults. Proper-noun density: 5+ per 200 words.

### Example 2 — Subtopic: "Does Astro support server-side rendering in 2026?"

#### Bad

> ## Does Astro Support Server-Side Rendering?
>
> Server-side rendering (SSR) is an important feature for modern websites. Astro is a powerful framework that supports SSR seamlessly, allowing developers to build fast, dynamic applications. With SSR, you can provide an enhanced user experience while also improving your site's SEO performance.
>
> Astro's SSR capabilities are comprehensive and flexible. You can leverage SSR for specific pages or your entire site, giving you full control over how your content is delivered. This makes Astro a great choice for businesses looking to take their web presence to the next level.

**Why it's bad:** every sentence is a forbidden default stack. "Powerful framework," "seamlessly," "enhanced user experience," "comprehensive and flexible," "full control," "take to the next level." No version number, no config example, no dated claim.

#### Good

> ## Does Astro Support Server-Side Rendering in 2026?
>
> Yes — since Astro 3.0 (August 2023). As of Astro 5 (late 2024), SSR is a first-class output mode alongside static generation; you pick per-route via `export const prerender = false`.
>
> To enable SSR you add an adapter — Node, Vercel, Netlify, Cloudflare, or Deno — in `astro.config.mjs`:
>
> ```javascript
> import { defineConfig } from 'astro/config';
> import node from '@astrojs/node';
>
> export default defineConfig({
>   output: 'server',       // or 'hybrid' to mix static + SSR
>   adapter: node({ mode: 'standalone' }),
> });
> ```
>
> **When to use SSR on an Astro site.** Dynamic data that can't be fetched at build (user-specific dashboards, real-time pricing, authenticated routes), or when the data changes faster than your build cadence. Most marketing and blog sites don't need it — static with incremental rebuilds is faster and cheaper.
>
> **The trade-off.** SSR pages don't cache on the CDN edge the way static pages do. A page that gets 10k hits/day will hit your origin 10k times on SSR vs 0 on static. For high-traffic marketing pages, stay static.

**Why it's good:** dated answer in the first sentence (Astro 3.0, August 2023; Astro 5, late 2024); concrete config example the reader can paste; named adapters; named-method decision rule ("when to use SSR"); contrast-and-claim close ("stay static for high-traffic marketing pages"). No forbidden defaults.

## §6 Self-check before handing a draft to the user

Run every item before showing the draft. If any fails, revise before presenting.

1. **Forbidden-defaults scan.** Ctrl-F each word from §1 (body-copy list, connective filler, SEO-marketer voice). Target count: **zero** hits. If one sneaks in, replace per §2.
2. **Specific claim per H2.** Each H2 section names a specific subtopic — not a generic directive. "How to Choose a Dog Trainer in Edinburgh" passes. "Important Considerations" fails.
3. **Dated claim presence.** At least one dated claim per section (a year, a month, or a specific Google update). If no timeline is in the section, the section has not committed to anything the reader can age-check.
4. **Proper-noun density.** At least one named proper noun (place, brand, product, person, credential body, regulation) per 300 words. Generic writing has zero proper nouns per paragraph; good writing has one per sentence.
5. **Meta-description keyword-frontload.** The primary keyword appears in the first 5 words OR is the first proper noun in the description. The description's first 80 chars contain at least one number, place name, or named method.
6. **Bullets-to-prose alternation.** No more than 3 consecutive bullets without a prose paragraph; no more than 3 consecutive prose paragraphs without a bullet list or table.
7. **No filler transitions.** No paragraph starts with a connective-filler phrase from §1. Every paragraph opens with a claim, a number, a proper noun, or a question — not with "Moreover," "Furthermore," or "When it comes to."

## §7 Field notes — what went wrong in past runs

Numbered, empirical drift observations. Each entry: one-line observation + the specific correction.

1. **Meta descriptions collapse to "Discover our comprehensive guide to…" when the keyword has no obvious specificity hook.** Correction: force a number, a place name, or a named method into the first 80 chars even if it means cutting the CTA. A description with specifics but no CTA beats one with a CTA and zero specifics.
2. **New H2 sections open by restating the heading.** Example drift: `## What to Expect in Your First Puppy Class` opens "Your first puppy class is an important step in your pup's training journey." Correction: cut the topic-restatement sentence, open with a specific number or scene ("Week 1 is 60% sniffing-and-settling, 40% name-game recall"). The H2 already told the reader what the section is about; the first sentence's job is to land the first specific.
