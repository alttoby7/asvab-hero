What GT actually is

The Army GT (General Technical) line score is the most leverage you have for picking your job. It comes from three subtests, in two steps:

VE = (WK + PC) × 2  →  GT = VE + AR

VE is your Verbal Expression score. The official scaled VE that lands on your record sits in the 20–62 range. Word Knowledge and Paragraph Comprehension both feed it, which is why every verbal point counts as much as an arithmetic point in the final composite. Most candidates ignore that and grind only AR. They leave free GT on the table.

The 4 levers that move GT

Stop studying the whole ASVAB to raise GT. Three subtests feed it. One more (timing) costs you points if you ignore it. Drill these four:

Lever 1 · Word Knowledge (WK)

  • Learn 15 prefixes and 25 Latin/Greek roots before any new word lists. Coverage beats memorization.
  • Drill under an 11-second timer per question. The CAT-ASVAB gives you about that long. If you can’t answer in 11 seconds at home, you won’t on test day.
  • For unknown words, use the “positive/negative/neutral” gut read first, then eliminate two answer choices, then guess.
  • Build a daily 10-card flashcard pile. Drop a card after you get it right twice in a row.
  • Read once at night, drill in the morning. Sleep is when vocabulary actually consolidates.

Lever 2 · Paragraph Comprehension (PC)

  • Read the question stem first, then the passage. You’re hunting for an answer, not absorbing the topic.
  • Underline the verb in the question (“implies,” “states,” “best title”). The verb tells you whether the answer is literal or inferred.
  • Watch for “extreme” words in answer choices: always, never, all, none. Right answers are usually moderate.
  • If two answers feel right, the more specific one is usually wrong. PC rewards the broader, defensible read.
  • Train on 4–6 short passages a day, not one long passage. Test passages are short.

Lever 3 · Arithmetic Reasoning (AR)

  • Translate first, calculate second. Write the equation in the margin before you touch numbers.
  • Memorize the 5 setups that cover ~70% of AR: rate-time-distance, percent change, ratio/proportion, work rate, and weighted average.
  • Estimate before you solve. If your final answer is in a different order of magnitude than your estimate, you misread the problem.
  • Skip and flag any problem that takes more than 90 seconds. Come back. AR rewards completion, not perfection.
  • Drill word problems, not raw math. Khan Academy SAT word-problem sets overlap ~70% with AR.

Lever 4 · Test-day timing & elimination

  • On the CAT-ASVAB you cannot skip and return. Commit to an answer in the time budget or your score caps.
  • Eliminate two wrong answers before guessing. A 50/50 guess is worth 4× a blind guess.
  • The early questions weigh more on adaptive scoring. Slow down on the first 1/3, speed up on the last 1/3.
  • Eat a real breakfast. Caffeine but not extra caffeine. Bring a sweater — MEPS testing rooms run cold.

The retake math: when is a second test worth it?

Retake rules apply to everyone. The pattern is the same across all branches:

AttemptWait before retake
1st retake (after first ASVAB)1 calendar month
2nd retake1 more calendar month
3rd retake and beyond6 calendar months

The score that counts is your most recent one. That cuts both ways: a retake can replace a low score, but it can also replace a high one if you walked in unprepared.

The Confirmation Test trap

If your retake score jumps 20 or more points within 6 months of the first test, the DoD requires a Confirmation Test (C-Test). Same test, same conditions, no warning. If your C-Test comes back lower, the C-Test score sticks. Crammers lose both scores this way.

The 5-point rule. If your GT is within 5 points of your goal, retake after a focused 14-day block. If you’re 15+ points off, take 60 days first — you’ll need both verbal and AR work, and a thin retake will just tag you for a Confirmation Test.

How much GT can you realistically gain in 30 days?

  • 5–10 GT points: highly realistic with daily 30-minute focused prep on WK/PC/AR. Most candidates hit this.
  • 10–15 GT points: realistic with 45–60 minutes a day, weak baseline (lots of room), and steady verbal work.
  • 15–20 GT points: stretch goal. Possible if your baseline was held back by test anxiety or unfamiliarity, not skill.
  • 20+ GT points: uncommon outside of the active-duty BSEP program (60+ classroom hours over 10 days). If you hit it pre-enlistment, expect a C-Test.

Active duty soldiers: take BSEP

If you’re already in the Army, National Guard, or Reserve with a GT of 109 or below, BSEP (Basic Skills Education Program) is free, on-duty, and averages a 19-point gain in 10 days. Talk to your installation Education Center. The pre-enlistment plan in this guide is the civilian equivalent — lower-intensity, but the same focus on verbal and AR.

One-page cheat sheet

Screenshot this. Open it the night before and the morning of the test.

  • Formula: VE = (WK + PC) × 2. GT = VE + AR. Verbal counts twice. Drill it.
  • Cutoffs to know: 100 (most MOS), 107 (technical / medical / intel), 110 (every officer pathway, 18X SF), 117+ (competitive SOF packets).
  • Highest-leverage subtests: WK and PC. A combined +5 = +10 raw GT.
  • WK pace: ~11 seconds per question on the CAT. Drill under timer from day 1.
  • PC technique: read the question stem first, then the passage. Underline the verb.
  • AR technique: translate to an equation in the margin before computing. Estimate first.
  • 5 AR setups: rate-time-distance, percent change, ratio/proportion, work rate, weighted average.
  • Elimination: kill 2 answers before guessing. 50/50 is worth 4× a cold guess.
  • Retake rule: 1 month, 1 month, then 6 months. Most-recent score is the one that counts.
  • Confirmation Test trap: +20 in 6 months triggers a C-Test. Crammers lose both scores.
  • Within 5 of goal? Retake. 15+ off? 60 more days first.
  • Day-of: real breakfast, normal caffeine, sweater, arrive 30 min early, don’t talk scores with other applicants.
